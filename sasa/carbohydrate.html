Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are an essential macronutrient that provide energy to the body. They are broken down into glucose, which is used by our brain, muscles, and organs to perform daily activities. Without enough carbohydrates, the body may feel weak and tired.

Carbohydrates are especially important for students and active individuals because they help improve concentration, memory, and physical performance. Choosing the right type of carbohydrates is very important for maintaining good health.

Beneficial (Healthy) Carbohydrates

Healthy carbohydrates are rich in fiber, vitamins, and minerals. They are digested slowly and help maintain stable blood sugar levels.

  • Whole grains such as brown rice, oats, and whole wheat bread
  • Fresh fruits like apples, bananas, and berries
  • Vegetables such as carrots, broccoli, and spinach
  • Legumes including lentils, beans, and chickpeas

Less Beneficial (Unhealthy) Carbohydrates

Unhealthy carbohydrates are highly processed and contain added sugars. They provide quick energy but have very low nutritional value and may cause weight gain and health problems if consumed frequently.

  • White sugar and sweets
  • Soft drinks and sugary juices
  • White bread and refined flour products
  • Fast food and packaged snacks

On average, these carbohydrate sources provide roughly 15- 30 grams of carbohydrates. To stay healthy, it is recommended to consume more complex carbohydrates and limit the intake of simple and processed carbohydrates.

Carbohydrate Deficiency

Carbohydrate deficiency can lead to fatigue, weakness, and difficulty concentrating. It may also result in the body using protein for energy, which is not ideal for muscle maintenance and growth.

Recommended Intake

The recommended intake of carbohydrates depends on activity level and energy needs. For most sedentary adults, an intake of about 3-5 grams per kilogram of body weight per day is sufficient. For individuals who exercise regularly or have higher training demands, carbohydrate intake should be higher, generally around 5-10 grams per kilogram of body weight per day, to support performance and recovery.
